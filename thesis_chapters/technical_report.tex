\section*{Abstract}
% =============================================================

This report documents the full technical methodology and results of a two-stage
master's thesis project on 3D human pose estimation for ergonomic risk assessment
in industrial environments.
Three pipeline directions were systematically evaluated against Xsens inertial
motion capture ground truth:
\textbf{Direction~A} (Sparse Keypoint Triangulation, SKT) achieves
\good{13.21\degr\ calibrated} / 18.59\degr\ uncalibrated joint-angle MAE
and 26.0\,cm MPJPE, with 76.3\,\% elbow RULA accuracy;
\textbf{Direction~B} (Dense Stereo SGBM, DDM) fails at joint locations due to
low-texture clothing and is abandoned;
\textbf{Direction~C} (Monocular MotionBERT, MTL) produces 218\,cm mean joint
distance, predominantly from coordinate frame misalignment rather than depth
estimation failure, and is abandoned.
The next milestone is real-time deployment on Viscando production hardware
(TensorRT + GStreamer/DeepStream).

\warn{Calibration caveat:} The 13.21\degr\ result uses piecewise angle calibration
fitted on the same single-subject (Aitor) recording used for evaluation.
Cross-subject validation is required before this figure is claimed as a
generalisation result.

\section{Introduction}
% =============================================================

\subsection{Project Background}

This master's thesis is conducted at Chalmers University of Technology in
collaboration with Viscando~AB, a Swedish computer-vision company specialising
in intelligent traffic and occupancy sensing.
The project goal is to develop a vision-based pipeline for real-time 3D human
pose estimation in manufacturing environments, targeted at automated
ergonomic risk assessment.

Data was collected at Viscando's partner facility.
The primary subject is \textbf{Aitor} (height 1.69\,m), performing a scripted
sequence of manufacturing-relevant activities: walking, sitting, squatting,
reaching, lifting, and interacting with dark furniture.
A simultaneous \textbf{Xsens MVN Awinda} inertial capture suit recorded
high-frequency 3D segment positions at 240\,Hz, exported as \texttt{.mvnx} files.
This constitutes the ground-truth reference throughout this report.

The project was carried out in two phases:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Stage 1} (October 2025 -- January 2026): Group phase with
        four members (Fanbo Meng, Zishan Deng, Jiahao Li, Huining Liu).
        Focus: camera calibration, 2D keypoint detection, stereo triangulation,
        and initial Xsens evaluation.
        Outputs: written report (January 30, 2026) and presentation slides
        (January 20, 2026).
  \item \textbf{Stage 2} (February -- April 2026): Independent phase (Fanbo Meng).
        Focus: SKT pipeline deepening, two alternative directions (DDM, MTL),
        and systematic comparison.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Research Objectives and Evaluation Metrics}

The ultimate application target is automated RULA (Rapid Upper Limb Assessment)
scoring, which assigns integer ergonomic risk scores (1--7) to body postures based
on joint angles at eight body segments.
This drives the metric priority order:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Joint Angle MAE} (\si{\degr}) --- mean absolute error over
        8 RULA-relevant joints (L/R Shoulder, Elbow, Hip, Knee) against Xsens GT.
  \item \textbf{RULA bin accuracy} --- proportion of frames where the estimated
        angle falls in the correct ergonomic risk bin.  A 10\degr\ error that
        stays within the same bin has \emph{zero} impact on the risk score;
        a 5\degr\ error crossing a bin boundary changes the risk classification.
  \item \textbf{MPJPE} (cm) --- mean per-joint position error; reference metric.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Fair GT protocol:} To eliminate measurement-protocol bias, all joint
angles are computed from Xsens 3D segment positions using the \emph{same}
geometric formula as the vision pipeline, rather than using Xsens native angle
outputs.  This ensures differences reflect pipeline accuracy, not formula
discrepancies.

\section{Stereo Camera Calibration}
\label{sec:calibration}
% =============================================================

\subsection{Calibration Target and Data Collection}

An asymmetric circular dot grid (5$\times$9 circles, centre-to-centre spacing
15.0\,cm) was used as the calibration target.
Two calibration video pairs were captured (\texttt{cap\_0}, \texttt{cap\_1})
under different board orientations and distances.
After detection, \textbf{40 stereo frame pairs} were obtained:
18 from \texttt{cap\_0} and 22 from \texttt{cap\_1}.

\subsection{Calibration Search Strategy}

Rather than tuning calibration parameters manually, a grid search was conducted
over solver configurations, with leave-one-pair-out cross-validation:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Reprojection rejection threshold: $\{0.35, 0.50, 0.70, 1.00\}$\,px
  \item Distortion model: rational model (14 coefficients) vs.\ standard (5)
  \item Intrinsic fix: \texttt{fix\_intrinsic=False} (jointly optimised)
\end{itemize}

Cross-validation protocol: train on \texttt{cap\_1} frames, evaluate on
held-out \texttt{cap\_0}, and vice versa.
The best configuration was \textbf{threshold~=~0.35\,px, rational model enabled,
intrinsics jointly optimised}.

\section{Direction A: Sparse Keypoint Triangulation (SKT)}
\label{sec:skt}
% =============================================================

\subsection{Pipeline Overview}

The SKT pipeline converts synchronised stereo video into 3D joint angle
estimates through six sequential stages:
\begin{center}
  Stereo video $\to$ 2D keypoints $\to$ Temporal smoothing \\[2pt]
  $\to$ Triangulation $\to$ Kabsch alignment $\to$ Angle calibration $\to$ RULA evaluation
\end{center}

Each stage is described in detail below.

% ── 3.2 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{2D Keypoint Detection}
\label{sec:detection}

\subsubsection{Final Model: YOLOv8m-pose}

The adopted 2D detector is \textbf{YOLOv8m-pose} (COCO AP $\approx 65$),
which runs independently on the left and right camera streams.
Output: 17-joint COCO-format keypoints with per-joint confidence scores.

Person identity matching across views (cross-view tracking) uses
confidence-score filtering combined with manual track labelling for the
single-subject scenario.

\subsubsection{RTMPose-x Comparison Experiment}

RTMPose-x (COCO AP $\approx 75$, a 10-point advantage) was evaluated as a
potential model upgrade.
Despite the higher benchmark accuracy, it \textbf{failed significantly worse}
than YOLOv8m on this dataset.

\textbf{Root cause of RTMPose-x failure:}
Both models use COCO 17-joint format, but their lower-limb joint localisation
conventions differ (different anatomical reference points for knee/hip centres).
In the wide-FOV camera setup used here, small keypoint offsets are amplified by
radial distortion, producing catastrophically large angle errors at the knee
(50--84\degr\ vs.\ 13--21\degr\ for YOLOv8).
The shoulder joints are less affected because the protocol difference is smaller
in the upper body.
This failure mode cannot be predicted from COCO benchmark AP.

\textbf{Decision:} Retain YOLOv8m-pose as the 2D detector.

% ── 3.3 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Temporal Smoothing: Adaptive OneEuro Filter}
\label{sec:smoothing}

Raw YOLOv8 keypoint detections contain significant frame-to-frame jitter.
A simple EMA (exponential moving average) applies a fixed smoothing weight,
which either over-smooths fast motion or fails to suppress jitter in slow motion.

The pipeline instead uses an \textbf{adaptive weighting} scheme that conditions
the blend weight on per-frame detection confidence and observed motion magnitude:
\begin{equation}
  w_t = \operatorname{clip}\!\left(0.18 + 0.52\,c_t + 0.22\,m_t,\; 0.15,\; 0.95\right)
  \label{eq:ema_weight}
\end{equation}
where $c_t \in [0,1]$ is the joint detection confidence and
$m_t \in [0,1]$ is the normalised motion magnitude at time $t$
(normalised by \texttt{motion\_scale\_px}~=~12.0\,px).

The smoothed position is then:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{p}_t = w_t\,p_t + (1 - w_t)\,\hat{p}_{t-1}
\end{equation}

Filter parameters (2D keypoints, applied before triangulation):
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \texttt{min\_cutoff}~= 1.4 (OneEuro base cutoff frequency)
  \item \texttt{beta}~= 0.05 (derivative coupling, reduces lag at high speed)
  \item \texttt{d\_cutoff}~= 1.0 (derivative filter cutoff)
  \item \texttt{motion\_scale\_px}~= 12.0\,px
  \item Weight clamp: $[0.15,\; 0.95]$ (never fully replaces or freezes signal)
\end{itemize}

The coefficients $\{0.18, 0.52, 0.22\}$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ema_weight} give a
base blending of 18\,\% raw (preserving some signal even at zero confidence),
a 52\,\% confidence-driven component (favours the new observation when detection
is reliable), and a 22\,\% motion-driven component (reduces lag when the
subject moves quickly).

% ── 3.4 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Missing Data Handling}
\label{sec:missing}

Keypoints are considered \emph{invalid} and excluded from all downstream
computation when:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item Full-frame detection confidence $< 0.35$ (parameter \texttt{FULL\_FRAME\_CONF})
  \item Crop-region detection confidence $< 0.20$ (\texttt{CROP\_CONF})
  \item Stereo pair confidence below $0.25$ on either view (\texttt{MIN\_PAIR\_CONF})
  \item Epipolar residual exceeds the adaptive threshold (see Section~\ref{sec:triangulation})
  \item Reprojection error exceeds the adaptive threshold
\end{itemize}

Invalid joints propagate as \texttt{NaN} through the pipeline.
Any frame where the required joint for an angle computation is \texttt{NaN}
is excluded from that angle's MAE evaluation (rather than imputed).

\textbf{Temporal window triangulation} (when enabled) partially fills sparse
gaps by pooling observations over a $\pm 3$-frame window
(\texttt{TEMPORAL\_WINDOW\_RADIUS}~=~3), requiring a minimum of 3 supporting
frames with stereo quality $\geq 0.35$.
Temporal contributions are weighted by an exponential decay with time constant
0.06\,s (\texttt{TEMPORAL\_WINDOW\_DECAY\_SEC}).

% ── 3.5 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Stereo Triangulation}
\label{sec:triangulation}

\subsubsection{Confidence-Weighted DLT}

Given a stereo keypoint pair $(u_L, u_R)$ with confidences $(c_L, c_R)$,
the 3D joint position is estimated via the Direct Linear Transform (DLT).
Each camera equation is weighted by its respective confidence:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{X}^* = \arg\min_{\mathbf{X}}
    \left\| \mathbf{A}\,\mathbf{X} \right\|^2, \quad
  \mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} c_L\,(u_L\,P_{3L} - P_{1L}) \\
                                c_L\,(v_L\,P_{3L} - P_{2L}) \\
                                c_R\,(u_R\,P_{3R} - P_{1R}) \\
                                c_R\,(v_R\,P_{3R} - P_{2R}) \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
where $P_{iK}$ denotes the $i$-th row of the projection matrix for camera $K$.

\subsubsection{Quality Filters}

Two adaptive quality filters reject unreliable triangulations.
Thresholds adapt to detection confidence so that high-confidence keypoints
are held to tighter geometric constraints:

\textit{Epipolar constraint:}
\begin{align}
  \tau_\text{epipolar}(c) &= \text{base} + \text{conf\_gain} \times (1 - c) \\
  &\text{with base} = 6\,\text{px},\quad \text{conf\_gain} = 18\,\text{px}
\end{align}
Frames exceeding the soft threshold (10\,px) are down-weighted rather than
hard-rejected, with maximum down-weight 0.80 and decay constant 3\,px.

\textit{Reprojection filter:}
\begin{align}
  \tau_\text{reproj}(c) &= \min\!\left(\text{base} + \text{conf\_gain}
                           \times (1 - c),\; \tau_\text{max}\right) \\
  &\text{with base} = 18\,\text{px},\quad
   \text{conf\_gain} = 42\,\text{px},\quad
   \tau_\text{max} = 80\,\text{px}
\end{align}

Additional hard constraints:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item Minimum disparity: 1.5\,px (prevents near-infinity depth estimates)
  \item Minimum pair confidence: 0.25 on both views
\end{itemize}

% ── 3.6 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Coordinate Alignment: Kabsch Algorithm}
\label{sec:kabsch}

The camera coordinate frame and the Xsens world frame differ by an unknown
rigid transformation (rotation + translation).
The Kabsch algorithm \cite{kabsch1976solution} finds the optimal rotation~$R$
and translation~$\mathbf{t}$:

\begin{equation}
  (R^*,\, \mathbf{t}^*) = \arg\min_{R \in SO(3),\, \mathbf{t}}
    \sum_i \| R\,\mathbf{s}_i + \mathbf{t} - \mathbf{g}_i \|^2
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{s}_i$ and $\mathbf{g}_i$ are matched 3D joint positions from
the SKT pipeline and Xsens GT, respectively.

\textbf{Implementation} (\texttt{shared/skeleton\_video\_utils.py}):
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Compute centroids $\bar{\mathbf{s}}, \bar{\mathbf{g}}$ and centre both point sets.
  \item Compute cross-covariance: $H = \tilde{S}^\top \tilde{G}$.
  \item SVD: $H = U\Sigma V^\top$.
  \item Rotation: $R = V U^\top$; if $\det(R) < 0$, negate the last row of $V$
        (reflection correction).
  \item Translation: $\mathbf{t} = \bar{\mathbf{g}} - R\,\bar{\mathbf{s}}$.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Elite-frame selection:} To ensure alignment is computed on
geometrically reliable frames, the \textbf{top-150 frames} with the lowest
lower-limb bone-length error are selected from the full sequence.
Reference anatomical leg segment lengths (hip--knee and knee--ankle, both sides):
$[38.6,\; 39.8,\; 40.3,\; 39.5]$\,cm.
At least 10 valid joint pairs are required per frame to participate in alignment.

% ── 3.7 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Time Alignment}
\label{sec:time_align}

Camera timestamps and Xsens timestamps originate from different clocks.
The temporal offset is estimated by cross-correlation of the vertical
acceleration signal derived from both systems.

Xsens segment positions are linearly interpolated onto camera timestamps
using \texttt{scipy\allowbreak.interpolate\allowbreak.interp1d}
(mode: \texttt{linear}, boundary: \texttt{fill\_value=NaN}).
Duplicate Xsens timestamps (if any) are deduplicated before interpolation.

The best cross-correlation offset found was \textbf{17.40\,s}
(search space: 17.20, 17.25, 17.30, 17.40\,s),
yielding a minimum mean error of 26.15\,cm MPJPE.

% ── 3.8 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Angle Computation and Fair GT Protocol}
\label{sec:angle_computation}

Eight RULA-relevant joint angles are computed geometrically from 3D positions:
L/R~Shoulder flexion, L/R~Elbow flexion, L/R~Hip flexion, L/R~Knee flexion.

For each angle, three 3D landmark positions define two bone vectors,
and the angle between them is computed via the dot product:
\begin{equation}
  \theta = \arccos\!\left(
    \frac{(\mathbf{p}_B - \mathbf{p}_A) \cdot (\mathbf{p}_C - \mathbf{p}_B)}
         {\|\mathbf{p}_B - \mathbf{p}_A\|\;\|\mathbf{p}_C - \mathbf{p}_B\|}
  \right)
\end{equation}

\textbf{Fair GT protocol:} The same formula is applied to Xsens 3D segment
positions to compute ``ground truth'' angles, rather than using Xsens native
joint angles (which use a different anatomical model).
This eliminates protocol bias from the evaluation.

% ── 3.9 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Angle Calibration}
\label{sec:angle_calib}

Two-level angle calibration was developed to reduce systematic per-joint biases.

\subsubsection{Global Piecewise Regression}

A piecewise regression curve is fitted per joint, mapping raw estimated angles
to corrected angles:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\theta}_\text{calib} = f_j(\hat{\theta}_\text{raw})
\end{equation}
Parameters: 10 angle bins, smoothing radius $r = 4$.
The calibration is trained on the Aitor recording and evaluated on the same
recording (in-sample).

\subsubsection{Quality-Aware Calibration}

To address the heteroscedastic nature of pose errors (high-confidence frames
are more accurate), frames are split by detection confidence at the 35th
percentile (\texttt{POSE\_QA\_\allowbreak SPLIT\_\allowbreak PERCENTILE}~=~35).
Each partition receives an independent calibration curve:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item High-quality frames ($c > c_{35}$): tighter calibration curve.
  \item Low-quality frames ($c \leq c_{35}$): softer correction.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Selective Application (Selective\_v2)}

Quality-aware calibration reduces overall MAE but slightly decreases
elbow RULA accuracy because the elbow's correction curve at high confidence
overshoots.
The \textbf{selective\_v2} configuration applies quality-aware calibration
only to Shoulder, Hip, and Knee joints, while the Elbow retains global
calibration.
This achieves the best trade-off (12.71\degr\ angle MAE, 77.27\,\% elbow RULA).

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{angle_calibration_comparison.png}
  \caption{Per-joint angle MAE under three calibration regimes.
           Quality-aware calibration improves most joints further, but the
           selective application (\emph{selective\_v2}) is preferred to
           preserve elbow RULA accuracy.}
  \label{fig:angle_calib}
\end{figure}

\warn{Overfit caveat:} All calibration curves were fitted on the Aitor
recording used for evaluation.
Generalisation to other subjects or environments is unvalidated.
Cross-subject or leave-one-out evaluation is required before reporting the
calibrated result as a deployment target.

\section{Direction B: Dense Stereo SGBM (DDM)}
\label{sec:ddm}
% =============================================================

\subsection{Motivation and Hypothesis}

Dense stereo matching (SGBM) produces a depth value for every pixel,
potentially providing richer depth information than sparse keypoint
triangulation --- especially for joints with low 2D detection confidence.
The hypothesis was that per-pixel depth from SGBM would reduce triangulation
errors for partially occluded joints.

\subsection{SGBM Configuration}

Semi-Global Block Matching~\cite{hirschmuller2007sgbm} was applied to
rectified stereo pairs.
Key parameters:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
  \toprule
  Parameter & Value & Rationale \\
  \midrule
  \texttt{minDisparity}   & 100\,px & Excludes depth $> 4.3$\,m \\
  \texttt{numDisparities} & 256\,px & Covers depth range 1.2--4.3\,m \\
  \texttt{blockSize}      & 9\,px   & Tested: $\{9, 11, 15\}$ \\
  Keypoint depth lookup   & $5\times5$ median & Robust to outlier pixels \\
  Depth formula & $Z = f_{x,r} \cdot T / d$ & $f_{x,r}=1037.6$\,px, $T=41.27$\,cm \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The depth range 1.2--4.3\,m corresponds to the disparity window:
any pixel with disparity below the \texttt{minDisparity} floor (100\,px)
is assigned the floor depth of $Z_\text{floor} = 1037.6 \times 0.4127 / 100 \approx 4.28$\,m.

\subsection{Fill Rate Analysis}

\textbf{Fill rate} is defined as the fraction of pixels with valid disparity
(i.e., $d \geq \text{minDisparity}$) within the bounding region of the subject.
Per-joint fill rates (mean across all frames):

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
  \toprule
  Joint & Fill Rate & Assessment \\
  \midrule
  R.\ Shoulder & 81.5\,\% & \good{OK} \\
  L.\ Shoulder & 80.2\,\% & \good{OK} \\
  R.\ Elbow    & 77.6\,\% & \warn{Marginal} \\
  R.\ Knee     & 76.3\,\% & \warn{Marginal} \\
  R.\ Hip      & 75.1\,\% & \warn{Marginal} \\
  L.\ Knee     & 72.8\,\% & \warn{Marginal} \\
  L.\ Elbow    & 48.9\,\% & \bad{Fail} \\
  L.\ Hip      & 39.4\,\% & \bad{Fail} \\
  \midrule
  Overall mean & 74.2\,\% & --- \\
  \bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The failure joints (LeftElbow, LeftHip) correspond precisely to body regions
covered by dark, uniform-texture clothing, where SGBM cannot find reliable
stereo matches.
Disparity values cluster at the \texttt{minDisparity} floor (100\,px),

\section{Direction C: Monocular MotionBERT Lifting (MTL)}
\label{sec:mtl}
% =============================================================

\subsection{Motivation and Hypothesis}

The hypothesis was that MotionBERT's temporal lifting of 2D keypoints into 3D,
supported by depth priors learned from large-scale training data, would serve
as a fallback for frames where stereo quality is poor (occlusion, subject
leaving the stereo baseline).

\subsection{Pipeline Architecture}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Person detection:} RTMDet detects the subject bounding box
        in each frame of the left camera video.
  \item \textbf{2D keypoint estimation:} RTMW produces 133-keypoint
        whole-body detections (COCO-WholeBody format).
  \item \textbf{3D temporal lifting:} MotionBERT (DSTformer architecture,
        42.5\,M parameters, pre-trained on Human3.6M) takes a sliding window
        of 2D keypoint tracks as input and outputs 17-joint 3D positions.
  \item \textbf{OpenSim integration (IK):} A TRC file of 3D marker positions
        was input to OpenSim InverseKinematicsTool (40~DOF model).
        However, IK-derived elbow angles proved unreliable, so all reported
        joint angles use \textbf{direct geometric computation from TRC markers}
        (same formula as SKT).
  \item \textbf{Evaluation:} Kabsch alignment and time-alignment identical
        to SKT.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{OpenSim coordinate frame:} X-forward, Y-up, Z-right.

\subsection{Preprocessing and Filtering}

The TRC marker positions output by MotionBERT undergo two filtering steps
before angle computation:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Butterworth low-pass filter} (cutoff 3\,Hz): A zero-phase
        4th-order Butterworth filter removes high-frequency jitter.
        The cutoff was reduced from an initial 6\,Hz (which caused
        over-smoothing) to 3\,Hz.
        Nyquist limit at 12.5\,fps: 6.25\,Hz.
  \item \textbf{Temporal median filter} (radius 3 frames): Applied to 3D
        marker positions before angle computation to suppress impulsive outliers.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Bone-length normalisation}: Disabled.
Per-frame scale normalisation is retained to handle the scale ambiguity
inherent in monocular 3D lifting.

an inverted coordinate frame.
This rotation error manifests as large horizontal and vertical ($XY$-plane)
displacement rather than depth-axis offset.

Correcting the input rotation would substantially reduce the 218\,cm joint
distance, but the direction was abandoned because SKT already provides
superior results without the rotation correction burden.

\subsection{Evaluation Metric Discussion}

\subsubsection{RULA Bin Accuracy vs.\ Angle MAE}

Mean angle MAE is a convenient scalar summary but does not directly reflect
ergonomic utility.
A 10\degr\ error within the same RULA bin has zero impact on the risk score;
a 5\degr\ error crossing a bin threshold changes the risk classification.
\textbf{RULA bin accuracy is the recommended headline metric for future reporting.}

\subsubsection{Fair GT vs.\ Native GT}

Using Xsens native joint angles as ground truth inflates the apparent error by
$\approx 5$--7\degr\ for most joints compared to Fair GT,
because the angle definitions differ between Xsens's segment-based model and
the vision pipeline's landmark-to-landmark vectors.
All results in this report use Fair GT wherever available.

\subsubsection{MPJPE vs.\ Mean Joint Distance}

MTL's 218\,cm ``MPJPE'' is not comparable to SKT's 26\,cm MPJPE.
MTL's figure is a mean joint distance that includes a large global offset
(pelvis displacement 207\,cm) caused by the rotation error.
True MPJPE (after removing the global rigid offset) would be substantially
smaller, but the rotation issue prevents fair comparison.

\subsubsection{Quality-Aware Calibration Trade-off}

Quality-aware calibration reduces overall angle MAE (12.54\degr\ vs.\
13.21\degr) but slightly decreases elbow RULA accuracy (75.6\,\% vs.\
76.3\,\%).
The selective\_v2 configuration recovers elbow RULA to 77.3\,\%,
providing the best operational balance.

\section{Lessons Learned and Discussion}
\label{sec:lessons}
% =============================================================

\textbf{Lesson 1: Calibration quality sets the ceiling, but single-subject
angle calibration may overfit.}

The 0.024\,px per-frame reprojection error of the Stage 2 calibration enables
metric-accurate 3D reconstruction.
However, the 5.4\degr\ gap between uncalibrated (18.59\degr) and calibrated
(13.21\degr) results comes from piecewise angle correction fitted on the same
Aitor recording used for evaluation.
The rebuilt pipeline achieves 13.91\degr, a 0.7\degr\ discrepancy from the
historical best, attributable to experimental-condition differences (lost original
pose NPZ file, random seed in top-150 selection).
Cross-subject evaluation is required before 13.21\degr\ is claimed as a
deployment performance estimate.

\textbf{Lesson 2: Fill rate is not a proxy for pose accuracy in low-texture
environments.}

A 53\,\% fill-rate frame had only 17.4\degr\ MAE; an 81\,\% fill-rate frame
reached 32.5\degr.
Dense depth coverage is irrelevant if the disparity signal at joint locations
is corrupted by low-texture clothing.
For ergonomics pipelines, accuracy at joints matters --- not average scene
coverage.

\textbf{Lesson 3: For monocular lifting, coordinate frame alignment matters
more than depth priors.}

MTL's 218\,cm mean joint distance is 8.4$\times$ larger than SKT's 26\,cm
MPJPE, yet only 34.5\,\% of that error came from the depth axis.
Planar drift dominates in 98.6\,\% of frames, caused by a video rotation
mismatch rather than monocular depth failure.
Integrating a monocular fallback requires solving coordinate frame consistency
first --- not improving depth estimation models.

\textbf{Lesson 4: The elbow is the hardest joint and the most critical for
RULA.}

Both SKT (30.7\degr\ uncalibrated) and MTL (54.9\degr) have their worst
performance at the right elbow.
The elbow is sensitive to arm pose relative to the camera axis.
At the same time, elbow flexion is a primary RULA Group A input.
Quality-aware calibration must be applied selectively to the elbow to preserve
RULA accuracy while reducing angle MAE on other joints.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}
% =============================================================

\subsection{Real-Time Deployment (Primary Milestone)}

Deploy the SKT pipeline on Viscando's production hardware using:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Nvidia TensorRT}: export YOLOv8m-pose to TensorRT engine
        (INT8 or FP16 quantisation); target $> 10$\,fps end-to-end.
  \item \textbf{GStreamer / DeepStream}: hardware-accelerated dual-camera
        capture with synchronisation; integrate triangulation and angle
        computation as C++ or CUDA extensions.
  \item RULA score streaming to Viscando visualisation interface.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Calibration Generalisation Validation}

Before the 13.21\degr\ calibrated result can be cited as a deployment
target, cross-subject evaluation is required:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item Leave-one-subject-out cross-validation (requires additional subjects).
  \item Per-deployment recalibration protocol (short 5-minute recording per
        new operator to re-fit the angle correction curves).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{RULA-Centric Evaluation}

Replace angle MAE as the headline metric with RULA bin accuracy and total
RULA score MAE.
This better reflects clinical utility and aligns with the application goal.

\subsection{Texture Camouflage Robustness}

The Black Chair interaction failure remains the most severe open problem.
Potential approaches: domain-adaptive fine-tuning of YOLOv8 on industrial
clothing datasets; background subtraction as pre-processing; active infrared
illumination.

\subsection{Shelved Directions}

Two diagnostic experiments were conducted but not advanced:
(1)~AFH1 hybrid pipeline (\texttt{04\_hybrid\_afh1/}): rigid transforms are
angle-preserving, so the hybrid did not improve angle accuracy.
(2)~Bone-length correction (\texttt{05\_bone\_correction/}): imposing bone-length
priors on triangulated 3D positions worsened angle MAE.

\section{Conceptual Architecture (Onboarding Reference)}
\label{app:arch}

\subsection*{Direction A --- SKT}
\textbf{Entry:} \texttt{01\_stereo\_triangulation/src/02\_batch\_inference.py}\\
\textbf{Input:} Synchronised stereo video (L+R) + \texttt{camera\_params.npz}.\\
\textbf{Pipeline:} YOLOv8m-pose on both cameras $\to$ adaptive temporal smoothing
$\to$ confidence-weighted DLT triangulation $\to$ quality filters $\to$
3D joints (\texttt{.npz}, $N\times17\times3$, cm, left-camera frame) $\to$
Kabsch alignment to Xsens $\to$ angle computation + calibration $\to$ RULA scoring.\\
\textbf{Output:} \texttt{results/optimized\_pose.npz}, evaluation HTML reports.

\subsection*{Direction B --- DDM}
\textbf{Entry:} \texttt{02\_dense\_stereo\_sgbm/src/06\_sgbm\_disparity.py}\\
\textbf{Input:} Synchronised stereo video + \texttt{camera\_params.npz}.\\
\textbf{Pipeline:} Stereo rectification $\to$ SGBM dense disparity $\to$
depth image $\to$ 3D point cloud $\to$ per-frame fill rate + quality metrics.\\
\textbf{Output:} Dense disparity maps + \texttt{results/interactive\_pointcloud.html}.

\subsection*{Direction C --- MTL}
\textbf{Entry:} \texttt{03\_mono\_motionbert/RTMDet-MotionBert-OpenSim/src/main\_inference.py}\\
\textbf{Input:} Left camera video only.\\
\textbf{Pipeline:} RTMDet detection $\to$ RTMW 133-joint 2D $\to$
MotionBERT temporal lifting (DSTformer, H36M) $\to$
TRC marker positions $\to$ Butterworth + median filtering $\to$
geometric angle computation $\to$ Kabsch evaluation.\\
\textbf{Output:} \texttt{results\_mono/} NPZ files + \texttt{skeleton\_comparison\_dirC.json}.

\noindent\textbf{Shared utilities:} \texttt{shared/utils\_mvnx.py} (GT parsing),
\texttt{shared/pose\_angle\_utils.py} (angle computation),
\texttt{shared/skeleton\_video\_utils.py} (Kabsch + visualisation).
